# Product & Ecommerce Copy

> Load this template whenever the user requests product descriptions, app store copy, feature announcements, FAQ copy, comparison pages, or testimonial formatting. Apply `copy-genie/hex-stack.md` (SPARKS) - every product copy asset should hit at minimum S (Sting the Skin) and P (Pitch the Prize) before moving to feature and mechanism language. Ground every angle in what Virlo data shows is working for ecom and product brands in the niche.

---

## Step 0: Research and Context Before Product Copy

**Pull Virlo data first.**

**Before writing ecom product descriptions:** Pull trending hooks from the relevant product niche. The hook formats driving views in supplement, fashion, beauty, or fitness content on TikTok are the same patterns that work in product ad copy. They're the same audience.

**Before writing app store copy:** Run a keyword search on terms real users type when they're looking for tools in this category. Those are your metadata keywords and your user language.

**Before writing comparison pages:** Pull creator data from the competitor's category using Orbit. What are they saying their tool does? What are users complaining about in comments? That's the objection and differentiation map.

**Confirm brand context.** Ensure you have the user's brand voice, audience, and positioning loaded (see SKILL.md Section 5, Step 1). Product copy that sounds like every other listing on the page doesn't convert. Product copy in the brand's voice stands out.

**Pick a source product listing or ad.** Find a top-performing product ad or listing in the niche (from Meta Ads intelligence or Virlo data). Break its copy into beats - headline, pain, mechanism, proof, offer, CTA. Build the user's version from the same structure using their product and voice. The proven structure stays. The nouns change.

## Product Descriptions

**The job of a product description:** Move someone from "what is this" to "I need this" in as few words as possible. Not a brochure. A conversion asset.

**The fundamental mistake:** Starting with the product. Starting with the customer's problem converts better than starting with the product's features.

**Headline:**
Benefit-first. What does this product do for the person who buys it? Not what it is - what it does.

- Weak: "Virlo Pro Plan - Advanced Analytics Dashboard"
- Strong: "Know what's going viral in your niche before you create your next video"

Apply the "which means" bridge to every feature claim:
- "[Feature], which means you can [direct benefit], which means you don't have to [current pain]."
- "Automated niche monitoring, which means you can walk into every content meeting with data, which means you never have to guess what to post again."

**Body structure:**

1. **Problem (2-3 sentences)** - Name the specific frustration. Apply SPARKS S. Stay at the specific or cinematic pain level - not general. "You spent three hours researching last week and still weren't confident in your brief" lands. "Content research is time-consuming" doesn't.

2. **How it works (2-3 sentences)** - The mechanism. What specifically happens when they use this product? What's the process? Apply SPARKS R (Riddle Me This) - the mechanism should feel new, not generic.

3. **What makes it different (1-2 sentences)** - The one thing competitors can't claim. For Virlo: the Virality Score, the niche monitoring automation, the research-to-creative pipeline in one place. Name it specifically, don't say "unlike others."

4. **Social proof (1 sentence)** - One specific number or result. Apply SPARKS A (Anchor the Assurance) - woven in directly after the differentiator, not dropped in a separate "testimonials" block.

5. **CTA** - One action. "Start for free at virlo.ai/?via=organic" or "Start your 7-day free trial." Never two CTAs.

**Format notes:**
- Mobile first. Most product pages are read on a phone. Short paragraphs. Bullet points for specs.
- Feature list: use when the reader is already in decision mode and comparing specs. Not the main selling copy.
- Specs: clean, scannable. Table format for multiple variants.

**Bulk generation:**
When a user needs 10-50 product descriptions from a template, establish the single template first - problem, mechanism, differentiator, proof, CTA. Then adapt only the domain-specific nouns for each product. This preserves structural quality at scale.

**Platform-specific conventions:**

| Platform | Key differences |
|---|---|
| Amazon | Keyword density matters. First 5 bullets are indexed. Lead with the primary keyword. Avoid claims that can't be verified. |
| Shopify | Full design control. Structure copy in sections. Imagery carries weight alongside copy. Longer is okay if it earns it. |
| Etsy | Personal and handmade positioning matters. "I made this" language. Story of the maker outperforms corporate copy. |
| App stores | Character limits strict. See App Store section below. |

## App Store / Play Store Descriptions

**Character limits (hard stops):**

| Field | iOS App Store | Google Play |
|---|---|---|
| App name | 30 characters | 50 characters |
| Subtitle (iOS) / Short description (Android) | 30 characters | 80 characters |
| Full description | 4,000 characters | 4,000 characters |
| Keywords (iOS only, not shown to users) | 100 characters | - |

**Title strategy:**
Primary keyword + brand name, within the character limit.
- iOS: "Niche Monitor - Virlo" (20 characters - room to work with)
- Android: "Virlo: Short-Form Content Intelligence" (38 characters)

The title is the highest-leverage SEO asset in the app store. The keyword in the title gets 3x the weight of keywords elsewhere. Pick the highest-volume keyword with the most direct match to what users search.

**Subtitle / short description:**
One line. The value proposition. For a cold reader who has never heard of the product.
- "Find viral content in your niche - before you create"
- "Niche research on autopilot for marketers and teams"

**Full description structure:**

1. **Opening (50-100 characters)** - Strongest hook. Bold claim or problem statement. This is what appears before "more."
2. **Problem paragraph (2-3 sentences)** - The pain the app solves. Specific. Cinematic if space allows.
3. **Key features in plain language (bullet list)** - What it does, stated as outcomes not feature names.
4. **Social proof** - One strong proof point. View count, user numbers, or specific result.
5. **CTA** - "Start for free" or "Download and start your first niche monitor today."

**Screenshot captions:**
Benefit-focused, not feature-focused. The screenshot shows the feature. The caption explains why it matters.
- Weak: "Niche Monitor Dashboard"
- Strong: "See what's going viral in your niche - updated daily"

**Keyword strategy (iOS):**
The 100-character keyword field is invisible to users but indexed by the App Store. Use it to include high-volume search terms that aren't in the title or description. No spaces needed between keywords - use commas.

## Feature Release Announcements

**Use when:** A new feature ships. The announcement is a marketing asset, not a release note.

**The rule:** Users don't care about features. They care about what the feature lets them do that they couldn't do before. Lead with the outcome, not the capability.

**Structure:**

**What's new (specific)**
Name the feature. Say what it does in one sentence that doesn't require technical knowledge. "Custom Niches now run on a daily cadence automatically - no manual trigger needed."

**Why it matters (benefit level)**
What does this change about the user's workflow? What can they stop doing? What can they now do that they couldn't before?
- "You set it up once. Virlo monitors your niche every day and surfaces what's working. The brief is already built when you wake up."

Apply SPARKS P (Pitch the Prize) at the cinematic level if it's a significant release.

**How to use it (quick steps)**
3-5 numbered steps. No more. If it takes more than 5 steps to describe, simplify the steps or simplify the feature.

**What's coming next (optional)**
If there's a logical next feature that this enables or sets up, mention it. Builds anticipation and reduces churn by signaling forward momentum.

**Tone:**
Excited but not hypey. "We shipped something useful" not "GAME-CHANGING FEATURE JUST DROPPED."

Vee can be the voice for feature announcements in the product and on Slack/Telegram. His voice makes feature releases feel less like corporate comms and more like a friend telling you about something useful.

## FAQ Copy

**The job of FAQ copy:** Answer objections before they form. A well-written FAQ is a conversion tool. A poorly written FAQ is a help document nobody reads.

**The core rule:** Answer the question in the first sentence. Then elaborate if needed. Readers scan FAQs looking for their specific question. They need the answer immediately, not a preamble.

**Bad FAQ answer:**
"Great question! When it comes to our pricing structure, there are several factors to consider. First, let's look at what's included in each plan..."

**Good FAQ answer:**
"All plans start with a 7-day free trial. No card required. After the trial, plans start at [price] based on credit usage and niche volume."

**Organization:**

Group FAQs by where in the decision process a user asks them. Don't organize by product feature - organize by buyer journey.

| Category | What they're really asking |
|---|---|
| Getting Started | "Can I trust this enough to try it?" |
| How it Works | "Will I actually know how to use this?" |
| Pricing | "Is this worth what it costs?" |
| Features | "Does this do the specific thing I need?" |
| Technical / Integrations | "Will this fit into my current stack?" |
| Support | "What happens if something breaks?" |

**Anti-objection design:**
Map each FAQ answer to the objection it dissolves. Money, time, and effort are the three universal objections. Every FAQ section should contain at least one answer that directly addresses each.

| Objection | FAQ angle |
|---|---|
| Money ("can't afford another tool") | "How much does Virlo cost?" → 7-day free trial, $0 to start, no card |
| Time ("don't have time to learn a new platform") | "How long does setup take?" → one niche monitor set up in under 5 minutes |
| Effort ("this will be complicated") | "Do I need technical skills?" → no API keys required for standard use, Vee walks you through setup |

**Use customer language in the questions:**
The question should sound like it was typed into a support chat, not written by a product manager.
- Corporate: "What is the data refresh cadence for niche monitors?"
- Real: "How often does Virlo update the niche data?"

**Link to deeper resources:**
For complex questions, answer briefly and link. "For a full breakdown of credit costs by feature, see the pricing page."

**Virlo-specific FAQs to always include:**

- What platforms does Virlo cover? (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Meta Ads)
- What's the difference between Custom Niches and Orbit Search? (Ongoing autopilot monitoring vs. one-time deep search)
- How does the Virality Score work? (Weighted score showing performance relative to the creator's own average)
- What's included in the free trial? (7 days, 100 credits, full feature access)
- Does Virlo work for [specific niche]? (Yes - if content is being made about it, Virlo can track it)
- Can I export the data? (Yes - PDF, Excel, CSV, JSON, Markdown)
- Does Virlo integrate with my existing tools? (Slack, Discord, Zapier, n8n, webhooks, public API)

## Testimonial Formatting

**The rule:** The strongest testimonials contain a specific result, a specific context, and a real person attached to them. Vague praise ("great tool!") is useless. Specific outcomes are powerful.

**Testimonial structure:**
1. **The headline quote** - Pull the single most powerful sentence from the testimonial and make it the headline. Bold it. Put it first.
2. **Context line** - Who is this person, what do they do, at what scale. One sentence.
3. **The story** - What their situation was before, what changed, and what specifically happened. 2-4 sentences.
4. **The result** - The specific metric if they shared one. Views, time saved, revenue, team headcount. Real numbers.
5. **Attribution** - First name, role, company or company type. Never fabricate. If they didn't consent to being named, use "a three-person SMMA team in London."

**Bad testimonial:**
"Virlo is amazing. Highly recommend to anyone doing content marketing."
- Attribution: Sarah M.

**Good testimonial:**
"We got our Sundays back."
- Marketing Lead, ecom brand, $4M ARR. "Before Virlo we were doing manual research every Sunday before the week's content brief. Now the brief is ready Friday morning. We saved 6 hours a week immediately."

**Format consistency:**
- Attribution always: first name + role + company type (or company name if approved)
- No invented metrics. If the number wasn't given, don't approximate.
- Quote marks around the direct quote. Attribution on the line below with a dash.

**Video testimonial script prompts:**
When asking customers to record a testimonial:

1. "Before you used Virlo, what did your content research process look like?" (establishes before)
2. "What specifically changed after you set up your first niche monitor?" (establishes the mechanism moment)
3. "What's one thing you can do now that you couldn't do before?" (the outcome)
4. "If you had to describe Virlo to a marketer who's never heard of it, what would you say?" (the one-liner)
5. "What's one specific result you can point to?" (the proof point)

Keep video prompts to 5 questions. Long prompt lists produce unfocused recordings.

**Placement logic:**
Testimonials perform best when placed immediately after a promise, not grouped in a dedicated "testimonials" section. Promise made → immediate testimonial → next promise. The proof is woven in, not staged.

If one testimonial section must be used, sort by specificity of result - most specific at the top. "We got 2.4M views from a content calendar built on Virlo data" goes above "really changed our workflow."

## Comparison Pages

**The approach:** Honest beats slimy every time. Sophisticated buyers research comparisons in depth. If the comparison page is obviously biased, they discount everything on it. Be accurate about competitor strengths. Win on the specific things Virlo actually wins on.

**Opening statement:**
Name the comparison directly. "Virlo vs. [Competitor] - what actually matters."

Establish who each tool is for in one sentence each:
- "Virlo is built for short-form content operators who need automated niche intelligence and a research-to-creative pipeline in one place."
- "[Competitor] is built for [their actual ICP stated honestly]."

**Feature comparison table:**

| Feature | Virlo | Competitor |
|---|---|---|
| Niche monitoring (automated) | Yes - continuous, daily cadence | [accurate answer] |
| Virality Score (per-video) | Yes | [accurate answer] |
| Creator outlier detection | Yes | [accurate answer] |
| Meta Ads intelligence | Yes | [accurate answer] |
| Content Studio (research to creation) | Yes | [accurate answer] |
| Pricing | From $0 (7-day trial) | [accurate answer] |
| API access | Yes - dollar-based billing | [accurate answer] |

Include things where the competitor is stronger. Readers already know this from their own research. Pretending it doesn't exist destroys credibility for everything else on the page.

**The core differentiator (own paragraph):**
The one thing Virlo does that no comparison table can fully capture. The pipeline: collect (Custom Niches, Orbit) → understand (Video Intelligence, Virality Score) → create (Content Studio, Vee) → deliver (Exports, Integrations). It's not a feature. It's a workflow that replaces 4-6 separate tools.

**Proof:**
One specific case of a team that switched and what changed. Real numbers. Named if approved. Anonymous company type and result if not.

**CTA:**
Free trial. No friction. "Start for $0 - 7-day free trial, no card required." Never "request a demo" on a comparison page. Decision-mode readers want to try, not talk.

## Quality Check Before Delivering

- [ ] Does every product description lead with the problem or outcome - not a feature name or product title?
- [ ] Is the "which means" bridge applied to every feature claim?
- [ ] Is there one and exactly one CTA?
- [ ] Are all Virlo links appended with `/?via=organic`?
- [ ] Are character limits respected for any app store copy?
- [ ] Do testimonials have specific results - not just praise?
- [ ] Does the comparison page include accurate competitor information - not wishful thinking?
- [ ] Is the SPARKS S layer (Sting the Skin) present in the problem framing? Is it specific or cinematic - not just general?
- [ ] Are any deprecated features mentioned? (If so: remove. No Global Niches, Media Generation, or Collections.)
- [ ] Is "start for free" / "start for $0" used instead of "sign up" or "subscribe"?
