# Ads & Landing Pages

Vee builds ad copy and landing pages from real market data, not from guessing what the audience wants to hear. The hooks that perform in organic short-form video are the same hooks that work as paid ad openers. The language your market uses in the top-performing content is the language your ads should mirror.

---

## Before You Write Anything

**Step 1: Pull Virlo data.**
- **What hooks are generating the highest views-to-followers ratios in your niche?** Those are your ad hook candidates.
- **What pain points keep appearing in top-performing content in your market?** Those inform your pain layers.
- **What outcomes do top accounts in your niche promise?** Those inform your promise layers.
- **What objections does your niche's content address most often?** Those inform your objection handling.
- **What Meta Ads are running in your vertical?** Pull Meta Ads intelligence to see what competitors are paying to push.

**Step 2: Confirm brand context.** Ensure you have the user's brand voice, audience, and positioning loaded (see SKILL.md Section 5, Step 1). Ad copy that sounds generic loses money. Ad copy in the brand's actual voice converts.

**Step 3: Pick a source ad or organic post.** From the Virlo data (or Meta Ads intelligence), select a specific high-performing ad or organic post. Pull its full copy.

**Step 4: Build the beat-map table.** Break the source into structural beats - hook, pain, mechanism, proof, CTA, whatever structure that specific ad uses. Map each beat to the user's version. Show the table before writing.

**Step 5: Write from the table.** Paid ads amplify what's already working. The beat-map ensures the proven structure stays intact while the brand's nouns and voice replace the source's.

## Meta Ad Copy

### Character Limits

| Field | Limit | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Primary text | 125 chars visible before "See more" | Full text up to 1,000 chars |
| Headline | 40 chars | Shows below the creative |
| Description | 30 chars | Optional, below headline |

The first 125 characters of primary text are the ad. Write those first. If the hook fails, the rest doesn't matter.

### The 10 Ad Structure Templates

**1. Before & After**

Hook: A sharp contrast between the painful before state and the desirable after.

```
[Before state - specific and painful]. [After state - specific and desirable].
[What made the difference].
[Proof: one specific result].
[CTA].
```

When to use: When the transformation is visual and measurable. Works best when the before and after can be shown in the creative.

Hook structure: Lead with the before state. The gap between before and after creates the pull.

Example primary text (first 125 chars): "6 hours of content research every Sunday. Then 40 minutes. Same results. Here's what changed."

---

**2. Insider Reveal**

Hook: You know something they don't. The hook establishes the insider position.

```
[Surprising thing insiders know that outsiders don't].
[Why the standard approach doesn't work].
[The mechanism only insiders use].
[Proof: one specific result].
[Exclusive access CTA].
```

When to use: When you have proprietary data or a unique perspective your audience hasn't heard. Pull from your Virlo niche monitor data - real numbers from your market are the insider reveal.

Hook structure: Open with the counterintuitive truth. Make them feel like they're getting access to something they weren't supposed to know.

Example primary text: "The accounts winning in [niche] aren't posting more often. The data shows something else entirely."

---

**3. Unexpected Discovery**

Hook: Something surprising was found. The hook makes them need to know what it was.

```
[Something surprising was discovered/found].
[It contradicts what everyone thought].
[The new evidence].
[What this means for them].
[Curiosity-driven CTA].
```

When to use: When you have real data that challenges conventional wisdom in your niche. Pull your outlier analysis from Virlo - what's performing that nobody expects to be performing?

Hook structure: Frame as a discovery, not a claim. "I found" and "we analyzed" outperform "you should."

---

**4. Social Proof Cascade**

Hook: Lead with a result. Then stack more results.

```
[Specific result - one number, clean and real].
[Second result - different dimension, same proof direction].
[Third result - ideally a different type of proof].
[Mechanism tease - what made this possible].
[Join them CTA].
```

When to use: When you have multiple strong proof points. Pull real numbers from your tracked creator data or niche monitor.

Hook structure: The first result is the hook. It must be specific, real, and impressive enough to make someone want to know how.

Example primary text: "One account in [niche]: 870K average views per video. 29K followers. That's not luck."

---

**5. Enemy Callout**

Hook: Name the villain - the old way, the broken system, or the approach that keeps failing.

```
[Name the enemy - what's causing the problem].
[The damage it's doing - specific and painful].
[The alternative approach].
[Proof the alternative works].
[CTA].
```

When to use: When your audience is frustrated with existing solutions they've tried. The enemy is never a competitor by name - it's the method, the approach, or the mindset.

Hook structure: Make the enemy feel real and specific. "Manual research" is an enemy. "Guessing what to post" is an enemy. "Doomscrolling TikTok hoping something sticks" is an enemy.

---

**6. Objection Destroyer**

Hook: Lead with the biggest objection your audience has, stated in their own words.

```
["[Their exact objection, in their words]."]
[Validate why they believe it].
[The counter-evidence].
[What's actually true].
[CTA framed as easy].
```

When to use: When your audience has a strong belief that's stopping them from engaging. Pull the most common objection from your market's content comments or from your niche monitor's topic clusters.

Hook structure: Putting their objection in quotes signals you've heard it before. It builds empathy before you address it.

Example primary text: "\"I don't have time to learn another tool.\" We heard that 200 times. Then we built something that runs in the background."

---

**7. Story Arc**

Hook: A specific moment in time. Something happened.

```
[The specific moment - time-stamped, visual, personal].
[The struggle that led to it].
[The discovery or turning point].
[The transformation].
[You can do this too / CTA].
```

When to use: When your audience is cold and needs to be warmed through narrative before they'll engage with a claim. Story lowers the guard.

Hook structure: Open with the scene, not the lesson. "It was a Tuesday afternoon" beats "I want to tell you about a time when..."

---

**8. Curiosity Gap**

Hook: Open a specific loop. Give them just enough to make them need to close it.

```
[Partial reveal - enough to create pull, not enough to satisfy].
[Deepen the mystery - one more detail that makes it more interesting].
[Proof that the answer is real and worth having].
[CTA to close the loop].
```

When to use: When the mechanism or finding is genuinely surprising. The gap only works if closing it delivers real value - otherwise it's a bait-and-switch and kills trust.

Hook structure: The hook must be specific enough to be credible and vague enough to be irresistible. "What I found when I analyzed the top 100 accounts in [niche]" is better than "a secret you need to know."

---

**9. Comparison Flip**

Hook: While everyone else does X, there's a different way.

```
["While everyone else does [standard approach]..."]
[The different approach - specific and intriguing].
[Proof that the different approach works].
[CTA].
```

When to use: When your approach is genuinely different from what your market is used to seeing. Pull from your Virlo niche monitor - if you can identify a format or approach that's outperforming the category average, that's your flip.

Hook structure: Lead with what everyone else does. The contrast does the work.

---

**10. Resource Maximizer**

Hook: Do more with less - less time, less budget, less effort.

```
[Resource constraint hook - name the specific resource being wasted].
[Efficiency promise - specific and measurable].
[Proof the efficiency is real].
[Mechanism tease - what makes this possible].
["More results, less input" CTA].
```

When to use: When your audience is time-pressed, budget-constrained, or burned out. This is the strongest frame for busy marketers and small teams.

Hook structure: Start with the constraint. "6 hours" or "$500 a week on research" is more powerful than "wasting time."

---

### CTA Types by Funnel Stage

| Funnel Stage | CTA Style | Examples |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Cold (awareness) | Curiosity-driven | "See how it works" / "Find out what's working in your niche" |
| Warm (consideration) | Value-first | "Try it free" / "Get your first niche free" |
| Hot (decision) | Direct | "Start for $0" / "Book a demo" |

Match the CTA to where the audience is, not where you want them to be.

## Google RSA (Responsive Search Ads)

### Structure

- Headlines: up to 15, max 30 chars each (Google selects the best combination)
- Descriptions: up to 4, max 90 chars each

Write at least 10 headlines and 3 descriptions to give Google's system enough variation to optimize.

### Headline Categories to Cover

Write at least 2 headlines from each category:

1. **Keyword-inclusive:** Contains the search term. "Short-Form Content Intelligence" / "TikTok Niche Research Tool"
2. **Benefit-led:** Names the outcome. "Find Trending Content in Minutes" / "Know What to Post Before You Post It"
3. **Proof:** "870K Avg Views. 29K Followers." / "Used by 1,000+ Content Teams"
4. **Urgency/offer:** "Start Free — No Card Required" / "First Niche on Us"
5. **Differentiator:** "Not a Scheduler. Not a CRM." / "Built for Short-Form Intelligence"

### Pin Strategy

Pin your most critical message to position 1 or 2 in Google Ads. For a brand campaign, pin the brand name to position 1. For a competitor campaign, pin the differentiator.

Only pin what absolutely must appear. Pinned headlines reduce Google's optimization ability - use sparingly.

### Keyword Insertion

Use {keyword:Default Text} to dynamically insert the search query into your headline. Useful for high-volume, intent-driven campaigns. Default text shows when the query doesn't fit the character limit.

## TikTok Spark Ads

Spark Ads amplify existing organic content. They perform best when the content already looks native - not polished, not corporate, not clearly an ad.

### What Makes a Spark Ad Work

- The hook must hit in the first 1-2 seconds. TikTok's auto-play means the first frame competes with everything else in the feed simultaneously.
- Native feel: Talking head, handheld camera, natural environment outperform studio production.
- UGC-style scripts (see Short-Form Video content template) are the highest-performing format for Spark Ads.
- Sound on: Most TikTok users watch with sound. Script for audio, add captions for the minority who don't.

### Spark Ad Script Template

```
[0:00-0:02] HOOK - pattern interrupt. First words spoken out loud.
[TEXT ON SCREEN: main hook message]

[0:03-0:20] BODY - one idea, delivered conversationally. Demo if relevant.
[TEXT ON SCREEN: key supporting point]

[0:21-0:30] CTA - one action, stated naturally. Not a voiceover announcement.
[TEXT ON SCREEN: CTA]
```

### What to Amplify

Use your Virlo niche monitor to identify your own organic content with the best views-to-follower ratio. That's your Spark Ad candidate. Content that's already performing well organically will outperform purpose-built ad content almost every time.

## LinkedIn Ads

### Sponsored Content

Professional but not boring. LinkedIn audiences scroll the feed the same way as any other platform - the hook still decides everything.

Primary text: 150 chars visible before "See more." Front-load the most important line.

Use pain-first hooks specific to the professional context: "Clients are asking for reporting you don't have." or "Your team is researching manually. Theirs isn't."

Avoid corporate language, stock photo aesthetics, and vague claims. Specific numbers and real outcomes outperform everything else.

### Text Ads

Headline: 25 chars. Description: 75 chars.

Use for retargeting - these run in the sidebar and perform best with audiences who already know you. Reinforce a specific message they've seen before.

### Message Ads

Sent directly to LinkedIn inboxes. Highest click-through of any LinkedIn ad format, lowest tolerance for anything that feels like a pitch.

Structure: Short personal opener → one specific value statement → one question or low-commitment CTA. No more than 4-5 sentences total.

## Landing Page Copy

### Above-the-Fold Requirements

Before the visitor scrolls, they must know:
1. What this is (positioning line)
2. What it does for them (outcome subheadline)
3. What to do next (CTA button)
4. Why to trust it (one trust signal: logo strip, testimonial, or data point)

If any of these are missing above the fold, the page is leaking.

Positioning line format: "[Category] that [primary benefit]." Or: "[What it does] for [who it's for]."

Subheadline: Expand on the headline with the specific outcome. Not "a better way to research content" but "know exactly what to post before you open a blank document."

CTA button: Action verb + specific outcome. "Start for free" beats "Submit." "See what's working in my niche" beats "Learn more."

### Section Flow

Every section has one job. The section flow is:

1. **Pain** - Make them feel the problem viscerally (SPARKS S layer)
2. **Mechanism** - Introduce the new approach (SPARKS R layer)
3. **Proof** - Stack evidence that it works (SPARKS A layer)
4. **Offer** - Show exactly what they get (SPARKS S/K layers)
5. **CTA** - One clear action

Repeat the CTA button after every major section. Don't make them scroll back to the top to convert.

### Objection Handling Section

Every landing page needs a dedicated section that addresses the 3-5 most common reasons people leave without converting.

Format options:
- FAQ: "Q: How long does setup take? A: Under 10 minutes."
- Objection/response pairs: "I already have a research process. / So did the teams who told us they got their Sundays back."
- "It works even if..." statements that dissolve specific constraints

Pull your objections from real conversations, support tickets, or sales call notes - not from guessing.

### One Page, One Goal

Every element on the page either moves toward the CTA or doesn't belong there. Navigation links that take visitors elsewhere, secondary offers, and unclear next steps all reduce conversion.

## Sales Page Copy

Long-form sales pages require all six SPARKS layers, hit multiple times, across a full narrative arc.

### Full Page Structure

**Headline section**
The headline is the most important element on the page. It must communicate the promise in the reader's language, create curiosity about how it works, and make them want to read the next line.

Sub-headline expands on the headline with specificity.

**Lead section (first 10-20% of the page)**
The lead must accomplish four things before anything else:
1. Capture attention (hook or pattern interrupt)
2. Expand on the headline promise
3. Establish credibility (quickly, without overselling)
4. Open a loop that makes them want to read more

The lead is not the place for feature lists. The lead is where you make them feel understood.

**Body section**
The body answers the reader's questions in the order they think of them:

1. What is this?
2. Is it new? Have I seen this before?
3. Who else is using it? (social proof)
4. How does it actually work?
5. Why would this work when other things haven't?
6. Is it hard to set up or learn?
7. How fast will I see results?
8. How much does it cost?
9. What if it doesn't work?

Use real answers from your market data. If your Virlo data shows tracked creators in your niche getting results in specific timeframes, those are your answers.

Each major section of body copy runs:
- The reader's objection at this point in the page (unstated, but present in their mind)
- A specific claim that addresses it
- 2-3 pieces of proof for that claim, each reframed to feel like a different angle
- The benefit translated into their life

**Offer section (last 20-30% of the page)**
Summary of everything: what they get, why it's different, what results they can expect, what proof exists.

Include:
- The complete deliverable list (named specifically)
- Any bonuses with assigned values (total should feel like significantly more than the price)
- Risk reversal (free trial, guarantee, or easy cancellation)
- Urgency element (real, not manufactured)
- The decision framed as a choice, not a demand

Framing the choice: "You can [start and get specific benefit]. Or you can [current painful state]. Or you can [try to figure it out yourself - here's how long that takes]."

Let them choose themselves into it.

## SPARKS Diagnostic for Ads and Landing Pages

Run every ad and every page section through SPARKS before publishing:

- **S (Sting):** Does the opening make the reader feel a real, specific pain? Is it cinematic enough to hit?
- **P (Prize):** Is the outcome of using this product specific, visual, and emotionally loaded?
- **A (Anchor):** Is there enough proof to match the size of the promise? Is proof woven in after each claim rather than saved for a "proof section"?
- **R (Riddle):** Does the mechanism feel genuinely new? Does the naming create curiosity without being vague?
- **K (Knockout):** Are the three universal objections (money, time, effort) addressed before the reader raises them?
- **S (Seal):** Is the offer clear? Is the CTA framed as a choice? Is there a risk reversal?

For a paid ad: S and R are the highest-leverage layers. If the hook doesn't sting and the mechanism doesn't intrigue, the ad doesn't convert.

For a landing page: all six layers matter. A is especially critical - pages with specific proof points (real numbers, documented results, screenshots) consistently outperform pages with vague testimonials.
