# Email & Outreach

Vee writes email and outreach copy grounded in real market intelligence. The best outreach doesn't feel like outreach - it feels like someone who understands the reader's specific situation reaching out at the right moment.

---

## Before You Write Anything

**Step 1: Pull Virlo data.**
- **What topics are generating the most engagement in your niche right now?** Your niche monitor shows you. Use that for nurture and newsletter content.
- **What are the top-performing hooks in your market?** The same patterns that stop scrolls in short-form video work as email subject lines.
- **What tracked creator data can you reference as proof?** Real numbers make claims credible. Round to clean numbers (870K not 873,214).
- **What does your daily report show about your own content performance?** Use your wins as email proof points.

**Step 2: Confirm brand context.** Ensure you have the user's brand voice, audience, and positioning loaded (see SKILL.md Section 5, Step 1). Cold emails that sound like templates get deleted. Cold emails in a real voice get replies.

**Step 3: Pick a source email or outreach message.** If the user has an email that performed well before, use it as the structural template. If not, select the email structure from this template that best matches their goal and apply the beat-map process.

**Step 4: Build the beat-map table.** Break the source email into beats - subject line, opener, pain observation, mechanism, proof, CTA. Map each beat to the user's version with their brand context and Virlo data. Show the table before writing.

**Step 5: Write from the table.** The structure is the proven part. The user's voice, data, and specifics are what make it feel personal instead of templated.

## Cold Email Sequences

Cold email is a four-touch sequence. Each email has a different job. None of them should feel like a pitch.

### The Four Emails

**Email 1: Pain observation**

Subject: 1-5 words. Curiosity or pain-recognition. Never clickbait the body can't deliver.

Opening: Observe something specific about their business, their content, or their market. This is the signal that you actually looked before writing.

Structure:
- Observation about their specific situation (1-2 sentences)
- Connect observation to a pain point common in their market (1-2 sentences)
- Mechanism tease - not a feature list, a hint at a different approach (1-2 sentences)
- Low-commitment CTA: "Worth a quick look?" or "Relevant?" or "Happy to share what I found."

Rules:
- 2-3 lines per paragraph, maximum
- No walls of text
- No feature lists
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- The opener is about them, not you

**Email 2: Social proof and mechanism tease**

Subject: Reference the first email or a new curiosity angle.

Opening: Reference the first email briefly, then go straight to the proof.

Structure:
- One specific result from a comparable account or market (real number from Virlo data)
- What made it possible (mechanism explained simply - one sentence)
- How this applies to their situation
- CTA: "Would it be useful to see this for your niche?"

Rules:
- Proof must be real. Pull from your tracked creator data or niche monitor results.
- One proof point, not three. One lands. Three reads like a list.
- Keep the mechanism tease - don't fully explain. Leave something for the conversation.

**Email 3: Case study or specific result**

Subject: Name the result or the time it took to get it.

Opening: Go straight to the story or the data.

Structure:
- A specific situation (market, problem, what they were doing before)
- What changed and why
- The result - specific, clean numbers
- How this connects to their situation
- CTA: "I can pull the same data for your market if you want."

Rules:
- Stories beat data tables. "One team told us they stopped doing Sunday prep entirely" beats "87% of users report time savings."
- If you don't have a real case study, use niche monitor data framed as market intelligence: "When I pulled the top performers in [their niche] through Virlo, the pattern was..."
- Never fabricate results or attribute outcomes to people or companies you can't verify.

**Email 4: Breakup email**

Subject: Short. Final. Low pressure.

Structure:
- Acknowledge this is the last email
- One sentence: the risk of doing nothing (what they lose by not engaging, not what they gain by responding)
- Door open: "No hard feelings either way. If timing changes, you know where I am."

Rules:
- Keep it under 5 sentences total
- Don't apologize for emailing
- Don't plead
- The breakup email often gets more replies than any other email in the sequence - because the pressure is off

### Subject Line Principles

- Curiosity or pain-recognition only
- Never promise something the body doesn't deliver
- 5 words or fewer - test shorter before going longer
- No "just following up" in subject lines (or anywhere)
- No all caps
- No exclamation points in cold outreach

## Welcome and Onboarding Flows

Onboarding emails are about activation, not education. The goal of each email is one specific action that moves the user closer to experiencing value.

### The Five-Email Arc

**Email 1: Here's what you just got**

Send immediately after signup. Job: orient and activate.

Structure:
- Welcome (one warm sentence, not a paragraph)
- What they now have access to (specific, not comprehensive)
- The single most important thing to do first
- CTA: that one thing, nothing else

Rules:
- No feature tour in Email 1
- No "we're so excited to have you"
- Get them to do one thing. One.

**Email 2: Quick win**

Send 1-3 days after signup. Job: deliver the fastest path to value.

Structure:
- Name the quick win (specific result they can get in under 10 minutes)
- The exact steps to get there (short, numbered, direct)
- CTA: "Go get it now"

For Virlo users: The quick win is setting up their first niche monitor. That's the activation moment. Write the email around that action specifically.

**Email 3: Deeper feature discovery**

Send 5-7 days after signup. Job: show what's possible beyond the quick win.

Structure:
- Reference what they've done (or what they should have done by now)
- Introduce one more capability - the one that compounds on the quick win
- Show the outcome of using both together
- CTA: "Try this next"

Rules:
- One feature, not a product tour
- Frame it as "what to do next," not "here's everything we offer"

**Email 4: Social proof and success story**

Send 10-14 days after signup. Job: reinforce the decision to stay.

Structure:
- A real result from a comparable user or market (pull from Virlo data or documented customer outcomes)
- What they did to get there
- The implication: if they keep using the product, this is possible for them
- CTA: "See your niche's data"

Rules:
- Use real proof. Pull from your tracked creator data or niche monitor results.
- Connect the proof to the reader's specific situation or market

**Email 5: Support and engagement**

Send 14-21 days after signup. Job: prevent churn from friction.

Structure:
- Acknowledge this is an early stage (most people haven't fully explored everything yet)
- Direct question: "What's been the most useful so far?" or "Is there something you haven't been able to figure out?"
- CTA: "Reply to this email" or "Book a quick call"

Rules:
- This email is for the users who haven't fully activated yet
- Keep it short and personal
- The reply CTA outperforms any link here

## Nurture Sequences

Nurture emails move prospects through awareness stages. Each email has one job: move them one stage forward. Not two. One.

### Awareness Stage Map

| Stage | What they believe | Email job |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| Unaware | They don't know the problem exists | Tell a story that reveals the problem |
| Problem-aware | They know the pain but not solutions | Name the pain precisely, open curiosity about solutions |
| Solution-aware | They know solutions exist but not yours | Introduce your mechanism as genuinely different |
| Product-aware | They know you but haven't bought | Proof, offer, risk reversal |

Identify which stage your prospect is at before writing the email. The language and structure change entirely based on stage.

### Rules for Nurture Emails

- One idea per email
- 2-3 lines per paragraph maximum
- Mix value emails (no CTA at all) with soft-CTA emails
- Never send two pitch emails in a row
- Value emails should be genuinely useful without the product

## Cart Abandonment and Winback

These emails use loss aversion as the primary frame. People are more motivated by what they'll lose than what they'll gain.

### The Framing Principle

Don't lead with what they get by coming back. Lead with what they lose by not coming back.

Wrong frame: "You're missing out on [feature]."
Right frame: "You've already done the hard part - building a research process. Without [specific thing], that falls apart."

The specific thing they lose should be tied to something they've already invested in or identified with. If they set up a niche monitor, they're losing the intelligence they've been building. That's more powerful than any feature list.

### Three-Touch Sequence

**Email 1: Reminder**

Timing: 1-4 hours after abandonment. Tone: helpful, not pushy.

Structure:
- Acknowledge they were in the middle of something
- Name the specific thing they started (not a generic "your cart")
- Remove a friction point if possible ("If you had a question, I can answer it.")
- CTA: Return to where they left off

**Email 2: Incentive**

Timing: 24-48 hours after Email 1. Tone: honest and direct.

Structure:
- Acknowledge they haven't returned
- Name what they're missing (loss frame)
- Offer something concrete to reduce the friction: a trial extension, a discount, access to a resource
- CTA: Take the specific action

**Email 3: Final warning**

Timing: 48-72 hours after Email 2. Tone: calm, low pressure, genuine.

Structure:
- This is the last email on this topic
- One sentence on what they'll lose
- Door open
- CTA: Return or don't - no pressure either way

## Launch Sequences

### Pre-Launch (Waitlist / Anticipation)

Job: Build anticipation and establish expectations.

Structure:
- Email 1: Announce that something is coming. Name it. Hint at the mechanism. Invite them to be first.
- Email 2: Share a behind-the-scenes detail or early proof. Deepen curiosity.
- Email 3: Final countdown. Remind them what they get by being early. CTA: "You're on the list."

### Launch Day

Job: Convert warm interest into action.

Structure:
- Lead with the announcement
- Proof (real result from early access if available)
- The offer: exactly what they get, how to get it, how much it costs
- Urgency: what's different about getting in now vs. later (must be real)
- CTA: The one action

### Post-Launch (Proof and FOMO)

Job: Capture late movers with social proof.

Send 2-3 emails over the open period:
- Email showing early adoption numbers or early results
- Email with a testimonial or specific proof story
- Close-cart email: deadline, final proof stack, last CTA

### Close-Cart

Job: Final conversion for fence-sitters.

Structure:
- This is ending [today/tonight]
- Stack your strongest proof in 3 sentences
- The loss frame: after this, the offer changes or closes
- CTA: one action, unambiguous

## Newsletter Copy

### Section Structure

A newsletter that feels like a chore to write will feel like a chore to read. Build each section around one clear purpose.

Common section types:
- **Lead story / editorial:** One topic, your take, your data
- **Niche intelligence:** What's trending in the market this week (pull from Virlo niche monitor)
- **Proof story or case study:** A real result, told as a story
- **Resource or tool:** Something useful with no pitch attached
- **Product section:** Brief, late, clearly labeled - after you've delivered value

Rules:
- Personality-driven headers (not generic labels like "Section 2")
- Value first in every issue. Product mentions come late - not before you've earned the read.
- Proper sentence case throughout
- Short paragraphs. White space.
- One clear CTA per issue

### Virlo Data in Newsletters

Your niche monitor is your newsletter's content engine:

- **Trending topics:** "This week in [your niche], the top-performing content was about X. Here's what that means."
- **Outlier breakdowns:** Pull a specific video from your outlier analysis and break down why it worked.
- **Tracking data:** Share a creator performance update from your tracked accounts, with context about what changed.
- **Trend digest:** Use the weekly trend digest as a starting point for your editorial section.

## Subject Line Banks

Generate 10-15 variations per concept before choosing. Test different approaches. The subject line is the entire email for most readers who never open.

### Curiosity-Based

- "the [thing] nobody in [niche] is talking about"
- "what I found when I looked at [specific data source]"
- "this was hiding in your niche data"
- "[number] accounts are doing this. you're not."
- "why [common belief] is costing you"

### Pain-Based

- "still [doing painful thing] manually?"
- "how long does your content research take?"
- "[specific frustrating scenario]?"
- "the real reason your content isn't growing"

### Social Proof

- "[type of account] averaged [specific result] doing this"
- "one account. [market]. [specific view count]."
- "what changed for [role] in [industry]"

### Urgency and Timing

- "this is closing [day]"
- "before [event/moment]"
- "[timeframe] to try this"

### Pattern Interrupt

- "weird question about your content"
- "i shouldn't be telling you this"
- "unpopular opinion:"
- "[surprising statement that contradicts expectations]"

### Rules for All Subject Lines

- Under 50 characters for mobile (most email is opened on mobile)
- No clickbait that the body doesn't deliver
- No "Re:" unless it's genuinely a reply
- Test subject lines against your open rate data - what's working, keep doing

## LinkedIn DM Sequences

LinkedIn DMs are 4-5 lines maximum. Anything longer gets ignored. Every line has to earn its place.

### Before Writing Any DM

Research the prospect:
- What have they posted recently? What topic?
- What company are they at? What's the company's primary motion?
- What role are they in? Are they a decision-maker or an influencer?
- Have they engaged with your content before?

The answer to "can we serve this person and how?" should be clear before you write the first word.

### DM Types

**Post commenter follow-up**

When someone commented on your post, they've already raised their hand. The DM acknowledges that and goes one step further.

Structure:
- Reference the post and their comment specifically (not generically)
- Ask one genuine question that extends the conversation
- Or: share one additional thing they'd find useful based on what they said
- No pitch. No CTA yet.

Example framework:
"[Name] - saw your comment on the [topic] post. You mentioned [their specific point]. Curious - [genuine follow-up question]?"

**Profile viewer follow-up**

They looked. You noticed. The DM acknowledges it without being weird about it.

Structure:
- Low-pressure opener that acknowledges the visit
- One relevant thing about their market or content that you noticed
- A question that opens conversation
- No pitch.

**Warm intro (you have a connection)**

You know them, or you have a mutual connection, or you've interacted before. The register is more personal.

Structure:
- Reference the connection or context
- One specific observation about their situation
- A low-commitment offer: "happy to share what I've seen" or "worth a quick 15 minutes?"
- No pitch in the first message.

**Cold outreach**

When there's no prior connection, the bar is higher. The DM must deliver standalone value.

Structure:
- Name something specific about their business or market that you've noticed
- Connect it to a relevant finding (from your Virlo data if applicable)
- One question or low-commitment offer
- 4-5 lines total, maximum

Rules:
- Never pitch the product in the first DM
- Never use "I" as the first word
- Lead with them, not you
- No attachments or links in the first message (triggers spam filters and reads as a pitch)
- 4-5 lines, hard stop

### Follow-Up DMs

Wait 5-7 days before following up. Send at most 2 follow-ups.

Follow-up 1: Add value. Share something new - a relevant data point, a resource, or a specific observation. Don't reference that you didn't hear back.

Follow-up 2: Brief and light. "Still happy to share [specific thing] if useful. No rush."

No third follow-up. Move on.

## SPARKS Diagnostic for Email and Outreach

Different email types hit different SPARKS layers. Know which you're activating.

**Cold email:** S (Sting) and K (Knockout) are highest-leverage. The opener must sting - make them feel the pain or missed opportunity specific to their situation. The body must knock out their instinct to dismiss it.

**Onboarding:** P (Prize) and S/Seal are most important. Every onboarding email should make the next milestone feel achievable and worth going for.

**Nurture:** R (Riddle) is the engine. Each email should deepen curiosity about the mechanism, not just restate the promise.

**Launch:** All six layers. Hit them in order: S (pain of the current state), P (the transformation possible), A (proof it works), R (why this is different), K (address the final objections), S (clean offer with urgency and risk reversal).

**Newsletter:** A and R do the most work. Real data (Anchor) from your Virlo niche monitor, wrapped in genuine curiosity (Riddle) about what it means.

Run the diagnostic before sending. If a layer is missing and it matters for the goal, fix it.
