# Long-Form Content

> Load this template whenever the user requests blog posts, newsletters, case studies, lead magnets, webinar scripts, or any written asset over ~600 words. Apply `copy-genie/hex-stack.md` (SPARKS) to every section before finalizing. Ground every claim, hook, and angle in data pulled from Virlo before writing - never generate from a blank page.

---

## Step 0: Research and Context Before Writing

**Pull Virlo data first. Always.**

- **For topic selection:** Run `get_trends_digest` ($0.25) or check an existing niche monitor (free) to identify what's driving engagement right now in the target market.
- **For hooks:** Pull top-performing video titles from a keyword search - these reveal what language and angles the audience responds to. Hooks that stop video scrollers stop article readers too.
- **For proof:** Pull Virality Score data, view counts, and creator outlier stats. Real numbers make long-form content credible. Generic claims don't.
- **For lead magnets specifically:** The data inside the magnet IS the value. Use Orbit search results, niche monitor findings, or creator benchmark data to populate it - not observations from thin air.

Minimum research requirement: at least one Virlo data pull before drafting. The resulting copy should reference specific findings.

**Confirm brand context.** Ensure you have the user's brand voice, audience, and positioning loaded (see SKILL.md Section 5, Step 1). Long-form content reveals voice weaknesses faster than any other format. If you don't know how the brand talks, every paragraph will sound like a different writer.

**Pick a source piece.** Find a high-performing long-form piece in the user's niche (article, newsletter, case study) and break it into structural beats. Map the user's version beat-by-beat before drafting. Long-form content without a structural source meanders. With one, every section has a clear job.

## Blog Posts & SEO Articles

**Pre-draft checklist:**
- Target keyword confirmed (phrase the audience actually searches, not a category label)
- Search intent identified (informational, comparison, how-to, list)
- Virlo data pulled for examples, hooks, and stats

**Structure:**

1. **Hook intro (first 100-150 words)** - Stop the scan with something they didn't expect. A specific number that surprises them. A claim that contradicts conventional wisdom. A scene that puts them in the exact moment they hate most. This section determines whether they read the rest. Apply SPARKS S (Sting the Skin) at the specific or cinematic level.

2. **Problem section** - Name the real problem, not the surface one. "Content creation is hard" is the weather report. "You spent four hours researching last week and still weren't sure what to post Monday morning" is the problem. Use specific language from the ICP. Reference what Virlo data shows about what's actually working in the niche vs. what most people do.

3. **Mechanism / insight section** - This is what makes the article worth bookmarking. The thing they couldn't have figured out without reading this. Apply SPARKS R (Riddle Me This) - create a gap they want closed. If the article is about what hooks are working in the supplement niche right now, the mechanism is the specific pattern Virlo data revealed - not generic hook advice.

4. **Evidence** - One strong proof point beats five vague ones. Specific view counts, Virality Scores, creator comparisons with real numbers. Name real findings. If research shows that POV hooks in the fitness niche outperform question hooks by 3x in the last 30 days, say that. Apply SPARKS A (Anchor the Assurance) - weave proof directly after each claim, not saved for a "proof section."

5. **Actionable takeaways** - What do they do in the next 24 hours? Not "consider your strategy" - actual steps. Numbered list, short items, no passive voice. Every takeaway should be completable.

6. **CTA** - One action. Match to the awareness stage. Top-of-funnel articles: "start exploring your niche for free at virlo.ai/?via=organic" or direct to the lead magnet. Bottom-of-funnel articles: book a call or start trial. Never two CTAs. Pick one.

**SEO mechanics:**
- Target keyword in: H1, URL, first 100 words, at least 2 subheadings
- Related keywords woven naturally throughout - no stuffing
- Internal linking to other content in the same cluster
- Meta description: 150-160 characters, includes keyword, includes a reason to click (benefit or curiosity)
- Subheadings every 250-350 words - readers scan before they read
- Paragraph max: 3-4 lines. White space is readability.

**Readability targets:**
- 5th-6th grade reading level (tools: Hemingway App, Readable.com)
- Short sentences mixed with medium sentences. No 40-word sentences.
- Simple words. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate."
- Read it out loud. If you stumble, rewrite.

**Length:**
- SEO articles: 1,500-2,500 words
- Pillar content / competitive topics: 3,000-5,000 words
- Listicles and how-tos can be shorter (1,000-1,500) if the format justifies it

## Newsletter Issues

**The job of a newsletter:** Build a relationship over time. Be the thing they look forward to. Product mention happens late - earned, not forced.

**Structure:**

| Section | Job | SPARKS Layer |
|---|---|---|
| Subject line | Get the open | S or R |
| Preview text | Reinforce the open reason | S or P |
| Opening hook | Pull them in immediately | S |
| Editorial section | Deliver the insight / story | A + R |
| Resource / tool section | Give something useful | P |
| Brief | Synthesize to one takeaway | P |
| CTA | One action | K + S (Seal the Deal) |

**Subject line rules:**
- Curiosity: open a specific loop they need closed. "what the top supplement brands know about Sunday morning content" is better than "content tips."
- Pattern interrupt: something unexpected in the context of their inbox.
- Never overpromise in the subject line - the body has to deliver what the subject implied.
- Test subject against the preview text. The two work together, not independently.

**Editorial section (the core):**
- One idea per issue. Not three. Not a roundup. One thing.
- Vulnerability before celebration. Sharing a mistake or counter-intuitive finding lands harder than sharing a win.
- Reference Virlo data to make the insight specific. "Our niche monitor picked up something this week that surprised us" + the finding = credibility + proof + curiosity in one move.
- Product mention: after beat 25 of the editorial section at minimum. Not in the first few paragraphs. Earn it.

**Voice register:**
- Proper sentence case (not lowercase).
- Internet-native but structured. Not corporate. Not lecture-y.
- Vee can appear as a source or contributor in the newsletter context - "Vee flagged this pattern in the supplement niche this week" - this builds his character without making the newsletter about him.
- Discount codes and exclusive offers: newsletter-only, named clearly, with expiry if relevant.

## Case Studies & Customer Stories

**The core rule:** Only real data. Only real quotes. Never fabricate outcomes, timelines, or metrics. If you don't have the real numbers, say so and ask.

**Structure:**

1. **Lead with the result** - Don't bury it. "A three-person SMMA went from 40 hours of monthly reporting to 4 hours - without firing anyone." The result is the hook. Backstory comes after.

2. **Situation** - Who they are, what they were doing before, what their day-to-day looked like. Specific. Make the reader recognize themselves in this person.

3. **Challenge** - The specific problem they couldn't solve with what they had. Reference the pain at the cinematic level - what the situation actually looked like, not just what category of problem it was.

4. **Solution / what they did** - The specific steps they took using Virlo. What features, in what order, producing what outputs. Not "they used the platform" - what specifically did they do with it.

5. **Results** - Specific numbers. Time saved. Views generated. Revenue attributed if possible. Virality Score improvements. Niche monitor data that led to specific decisions. The more specific, the more believable.

6. **Takeaway** - What the reader should take from this and apply to their own situation. One clear learning.

**Proof formatting:**
- Pull exact quotes where available. Don't paraphrase unless necessary.
- Include Virlo screenshots or data export visuals if the user can provide them.
- Before/after comparisons with real numbers are the strongest proof format.
- Attribute correctly: "a marketing team at [company type]" is fine if you can't name the client. Never name anyone who hasn't approved.

## Lead Magnets

**The test of a good lead magnet:** Would someone pay for this? If the answer is no, it's not good enough to gate.

**Title formula:** [Specific number/claim] + [Specific audience] + [Specific mechanism]
- Bad: "Content Research Guide"
- Good: "The 14-Day Viral Hook Index: What's Working in the Supplement Niche Right Now"
- The title promises something specific that cannot be found anywhere else.

**Types and what to use Virlo data for:**

| Magnet type | Virlo data to pull |
|---|---|
| Niche intelligence report | Orbit search results, top creators by Virality Score, trending hooks |
| Hook swipe file | Top-performing video titles from niche keyword search |
| Competitor breakdown | Creator lookup data, posting cadence, outlier analysis |
| Trend report | Trend digest, hashtag performance, rising formats |
| Template kit | Hook patterns extracted from top-performing video data |

**Structure:**
1. Context - Why this information matters right now. Reference what's happening in the niche.
2. Methodology - How you found what you found (Virlo niche data, Orbit search, creator tracking).
3. Findings - The actual data, clearly presented. This is the product.
4. Actionable insights - What to do with the findings.
5. About Virlo - Brief, at the end. One paragraph. "How we found this" naturally leads to "here's the tool."

**Design and formatting:**
- Proper sentence case. Headers in Title Case.
- Section markers and emoji numbering for scannability.
- Bold key terms, metrics, and product names.
- Hyperlink Virlo with `?via=organic` suffix: [Virlo](https://virlo.ai/?via=organic)
- "We" language throughout. This is Virlo's data.

**Gate strategy:**
- Free tier: enough to prove the value of the data and make them want more.
- Gated tier: the deeper analysis, templates, or export file.
- The email they give for the magnet goes into a nurture sequence - not a one-and-done delivery.

## Webinar & Podcast Scripts

**Platform differences:**
- Webinar: structured progression, slides, demos possible, CTA at end
- Podcast: conversational, no slides, story-heavy, CTA can be woven throughout

**Webinar structure:**

| Segment | Time | SPARKS | Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hook | 0-2 min | S | Stop them from multitasking |
| Credibility + agenda | 2-4 min | A | Earn their attention for the next 60 minutes |
| Problem section | 4-8 min | S | Make the pain specific and shared |
| Market insight | 8-12 min | R | The thing they couldn't have known without being here |
| Mechanism reveal | 12-18 min | R | How Virlo solves what they've been trying to solve manually |
| Proof / demo | 18-28 min | A | Show it working on real niche data |
| Objection handling | 28-32 min | K | Address money/time/effort before they silently leave |
| Offer | 32-38 min | S (Seal) | Clear CTA, value stack, trial details |
| Q&A | 38-50 min | A | More proof in conversational form |

**Re-engagement triggers:** Open a new loop or introduce a proof point every 60-90 seconds. Attention drops consistently at 90-second intervals without a new hook.

**Speaker notes format:**
- [SLIDE: title] before each new visual
- [PAUSE - let this land] after a strong pain or proof statement
- [DEMO - show niche monitor setup] for live product moments
- [B-ROLL: niche data dashboard] for visual callouts

**Podcast script format:**
- Write like you talk. Read every line out loud. Fix anything you stumble on.
- Shorter sentences than written content. More fragments allowed.
- Story arcs over feature explanations. "Here's what happened when a team ran this on their supplement niche" beats "here's how Orbit works."
- CTA woven in twice: mid-episode ("virlo.ai/?via=organic to get your first niche free") and end.

## Long-Form YouTube Scripts

**Structure:**

| Segment | Time | Job |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | 0-30 sec | Stop the click-away. Open a loop immediately. |
| Intro loop | 30-60 sec | Tell them exactly what they're getting. Make them stay. |
| Problem | 1-3 min | Build the shared pain. Cinematic, specific. |
| Credibility / why listen to me | 3-4 min | Brief. One proof point. Move on. |
| Main content | 4-12 min | The methodology, findings, or demonstration. |
| Proof integration | Woven throughout | Screenshots, numbers, real examples from Virlo data. |
| CTA midpoint | Around 6 min | "If you're getting value, subscribe" or "link below." |
| Closer | 12-14 min | Single strongest takeaway. |
| CTA end | 14-15 min | One action. Never three. |

**Hook formats that convert (from Virlo video data patterns):**
- Bold counter-intuitive claim: "Posting more often will shrink your reach. Here's what the data shows."
- Specific result: "This one hook type drove 847K views for a 4K-subscriber account last month."
- Direct address: "If you're an SMMA and you're still researching by scrolling TikTok, this is for you."
- POV opener: "I pulled the last 30 days of data from 200 creators in the supplement niche. This is what I found."

**Script formatting:**
- [CUT TO: screen recording] for demos
- [TEXT ON SCREEN: the key stat] for emphasis
- [B-ROLL: dashboard view] for visual transitions
- Mark pauses. Mark emphasis words. This makes recording faster.

## Product Hunt & Launch Copy

**Tagline (for PH title):** One line. What it does + who it's for. No jargon. No "AI-powered." Specific.
- Weak: "AI-powered short-form content intelligence"
- Strong: "Find out what's going viral in your niche before you hit record"

**Product Hunt description structure:**
1. Open with the problem in 1-2 sentences (not your product name)
2. Introduce Virlo as the solution with the mechanism named specifically
3. Key features in plain language - what they do, not what they're called
4. One proof point (specific number)
5. CTA: start for free

**First comment (founder voice):**
- Why you built it: specific story, not a mission statement
- What makes it different: the one thing competitors can't claim
- What you want feedback on: a specific question keeps comments rolling

**Maker comment strategy:**
- Respond to every comment in the first 4 hours
- When someone asks about a feature, answer and add a use case they didn't think of
- When someone mentions a competitor, acknowledge it and explain the specific difference calmly

## Comparison Pages

**Positioning rule:** Be honest about where you win and where you don't. Readers are sophisticated. Dishonest comparisons destroy trust.

**Structure:**

1. **Opening statement** - Name the comparison directly. "Virlo vs. [Competitor] - what's the real difference."

2. **Who each tool is for** - Two clear avatars. Virlo's avatar: short-form content operators who need automated niche intelligence, not manual tracking. Competitor's avatar: stated honestly.

3. **Feature comparison table** - Side-by-side. Include the things Virlo wins on AND the areas where the competitor has a stronger feature. Honesty builds credibility.

4. **The core differentiator** - One paragraph on what makes Virlo genuinely different. Not a list. Not "we're better." The mechanism: continuous automated niche monitoring, Virality Score at the video level, research-to-creative pipeline in one place.

5. **Proof** - Specific example of a team that switched and what changed.

6. **CTA** - Free trial. No friction. "Start for $0, no card required."

## Quality Check Before Delivering

Run every long-form piece through these before handing it to the user:

- [ ] Is there at least one piece of real Virlo data in the piece? (specific view count, Virality Score, niche finding, creator metric)
- [ ] Does the hook stop you? Would you keep reading if you hit this on a scroll?
- [ ] Is the problem specific enough? (Cinematic or emotional level, not just general)
- [ ] Is proof woven in directly after claims - not saved for a block section?
- [ ] Is there exactly one CTA?
- [ ] Read out loud: did you stumble anywhere? Fix it.
- [ ] SPARKS audit: which layers are present? Is any layer completely absent that should be there?
- [ ] No fabricated quotes, outcomes, or metrics.
