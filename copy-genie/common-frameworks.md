# Common Frameworks

Frameworks are the scaffolding underneath good copy. They give structure to the thinking before the writing starts. The best copywriters don't pick one framework and worship it - they know several and layer them together depending on the job.

What follows is a practical guide to the frameworks Vee reaches for most. Each one includes what it is, when it works best, the structure, and a quick example.

---

## PAS - Problem → Agitate → Solve

**What it is:** The workhorse of direct response copy. Name the problem in the reader's language, make it worse before you offer relief, then introduce your solution as the way out.

**When to use it:** Short-form ads, cold emails, landing page heroes, hooks, any situation where you need to move fast.

**Structure:**
1. Name the problem in their exact words
2. Agitate - go deeper, more specific, more painful
3. Introduce the solution

**Example:**
> Managing content research takes way too long. You're buried in tabs, chasing down videos that probably won't even be relevant by the time you brief your team - and you're doing this every week, on repeat, for every client, with zero system. Virlo automates the whole thing. Your niche's top-performing content, organized and updated daily, without you touching it.

**Note:** The agitation step is where most people pull their punches. Don't. The more vividly you describe the pain, the more relief the solution provides.

## AIDA - Attention → Interest → Desire → Action

**What it is:** The classic four-stage conversion arc. Gets the reader's attention, holds it by building relevance, converts attention into desire through outcomes and proof, then channels that desire into one specific action.

**When to use it:** Sales pages, VSLs, email sequences, webinar scripts, any longer-form piece where you have room to build.

**Structure:**
1. Attention - something unexpected, a pattern interrupt, a bold claim
2. Interest - relevance, context, why this matters to them specifically
3. Desire - outcomes, proof, mechanism, the "after" picture
4. Action - one clear next step

**Example:**
> The agencies growing fastest in 2025 aren't hiring bigger teams. [Attention] They've figured out how to get research that used to take a week done in under an hour. [Interest] Their clients are seeing content ideas that are already proven to work in their market, with the data to back it up. [Desire] Start your free trial today. [Action]

**Note:** Most copy fails at Desire. It jumps from Interest straight to Action without building enough want. Desire is where you spend the most time on a longer piece.

## 4Ps - Promise → Picture → Proof → Push

**What it is:** Lead with a bold outcome, paint the vivid after-state, stack proof, then push to action with urgency. Front-loads the benefit to hook skimmers.

**When to use it:** Landing pages, offer pages, product launch announcements.

**Structure:**
1. Promise - the boldest, most specific version of the outcome
2. Picture - what that outcome looks like in their real life
3. Proof - why they should believe it
4. Push - a clear, urgent call to action

**Example:**
> Cut your content research from six hours to forty minutes. [Promise] Wake up Monday with your niche's top performers already sorted, tagged, and ready to brief - before your first coffee. [Picture] Over 3,000 marketers and agencies are already running on this workflow. [Proof] Start free today - no card required. [Push]

**Note:** The Picture step is where this framework shines over PAS. It's optimistic and forward-looking. Use 4Ps when you're writing to people who are solution-aware and ready to be convinced - not when they first need to feel understood.

## Before → After → Bridge

**What it is:** Contrast is one of the most powerful persuasion tools available. Show the painful current state, show the desired state, then position your product as the thing that gets them from one to the other.

**When to use it:** Social posts, email hooks, case study framing, any short piece where you need to communicate transformation quickly.

**Structure:**
1. Before - the current painful situation, described specifically
2. After - the desired situation, described vividly
3. Bridge - your product or approach is how they get from Before to After

**Example:**
> Six hours a week digging through competitor accounts, taking screenshots, trying to remember which video had that hook format your client asked about. [Before] One dashboard. All of it automated, organized, and searchable. New content surfaced daily. [After] That's what Virlo does. [Bridge]

**Note:** The Before state must be genuinely painful and specific. Generic Befores produce generic copy. The more precisely you describe someone's actual Tuesday at 10pm, the more the After resonates.

## The Value Equation

**What it is:** A diagnostic lens that measures the perceived value of any offer. Not a writing structure - a filter you run every line of copy through.

**The formula:**
> Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort and Sacrifice)

**What this means in practice:**

To increase value, you can either increase the top of the fraction or decrease the bottom.

- **Increase Dream Outcome:** Make the result bigger, more specific, more emotionally loaded. Connect it to how others will perceive them.
- **Increase Perceived Likelihood:** Add proof, testimonials, track records, demonstrations, guarantees. Make it feel like something that reliably happens.
- **Decrease Time Delay:** Give specific, fast timeframes. "In 7 days" beats "quickly." "This afternoon" beats "soon."
- **Decrease Effort and Sacrifice:** Make it feel low-lift. Reduce setup, reduce learning curve, reduce what they have to give up.

**When to use it:** After drafting. Run every major claim through these four levers. If a line doesn't move at least one of them, it probably doesn't need to be there.

## Customer Awareness Levels

**What it is:** A map of where any reader sits in their relationship to the problem and your solution. Your copy must match their current level or it won't land.

**The five levels:**

| Level | Where they are | How to lead |
|---|---|---|
| Unaware | Don't know they have a problem | Story, shock, pattern interrupt |
| Problem-Aware | Know the pain, don't know solutions exist | Lead with pain and empathy |
| Solution-Aware | Know solutions exist, don't know yours | Lead with mechanism and differentiation |
| Product-Aware | Know your product, haven't bought | Lead with proof, offer, risk reversal |
| Most Aware | Know you, trust you | Lead with the deal, direct CTA |

**The rule:** Move them one stage at a time. You can't write Most Aware copy to an Unaware audience. You'll confuse them. You also can't write Unaware copy to someone ready to buy - you'll bore them.

**Practical application:** Cold social traffic is almost always Problem-Aware at best. Email to your list is Solution-Aware or Product-Aware. Retargeting ads go to Product-Aware. A sign-up confirmation email goes to Most Aware.

## Life Force 8

**What it is:** Eight hardwired biological desires that every human carries. The most powerful copy taps directly into at least one of them.

**The eight:**
1. Survival, enjoyment of life, life extension
2. Enjoyment of food and beverages
3. Freedom from fear, pain, and danger
4. Sexual companionship
5. Comfortable living conditions
6. To be superior, winning, keeping up with peers
7. Care and protection of loved ones
8. Social approval

For marketing software and SaaS, the most relevant forces are typically 3 (freedom from fear and pain), 6 (superiority and winning), and 8 (social approval and peer perception).

**How to use it:** Before writing, identify which Life Force your product actually touches. Then build your emotional layer around that force rather than generic "save time" or "grow revenue" language.

> Touching LF6: "The agencies that master content intelligence first will be the ones their clients can't imagine leaving." (superiority, winning)
> Touching LF3: "Stop guessing what to post and feeling like the one person who doesn't have a system." (freedom from fear, from pain)

**Note:** The two strongest universal appeals across almost every market are superiority/winning (LF6) and social approval (LF8). If in doubt, one of these is usually the right emotional layer.

## NESB - New, Easy, Safe, Big

**What it is:** Four emotional drivers that every piece of copy should communicate at least one of. Think of them as the "alter egos" of perceived value.

**N - New (alter ego: Only)**

The most important of the four. If the reader thinks they've seen this before, they leave. Categorization is death in copy. New offers hope that this might finally be the thing that works - even when everything else didn't.

> Power words: breakthrough, first-of-its-kind, announcing, revealed, introducing, only
> The goal is not "better." It's "different."

**E - Easy (alter ego: Anybody)**

People want results without complexity. Easy's alter ego goes deeper than convenience - it tackles the reader's insecurity that they're "not technical enough" or "not smart enough."

> "You don't need to be a data scientist. You don't need a dedicated analyst. You don't need any technical setup."
> Power words: simple, automatic, one-click, step-by-step, no experience required

**S - Safe (alter ego: Predictable)**

Loss aversion is real. People care more about not losing something they have than gaining something they don't. Safe copy leads with track records, repeated results, and historical patterns - not just promises.

> "847 agencies already run their research this way" beats "agencies love it."
> Specific track records ("119 wins, 0 losses") beat percentages ("100% success rate" - percentages increase skepticism).

**B - Big (alter ego: Fast)**

Big opportunities demand attention. Big implies change. Big works well with urgency and FOMO. Fast multiplies Big - "grow 10x" is interesting, "grow 10x in 90 days" is irresistible.

> Power words: massive, once-in-a-generation, now, today, overnight, this week
> Combine with FOMO: "Your competitors figured this out six months ago."

**How to use NESB:** Before writing any piece of copy, ask: which of these four am I primarily communicating? If the answer is "none clearly," the copy will feel flat. Every strong piece of persuasive content leads with at least one NESB driver and reinforces it throughout.

## A Note on Using Frameworks

These are lenses, not templates.

Using one framework robotically produces copy that feels mechanical. The frameworks are for structuring your thinking before you write - not for filling in blanks.

The most effective copy layers 2-3 frameworks simultaneously. PAS sets the structure. NESB sets the emotional driver. The Value Equation filters every line. Awareness level calibrates the tone and starting point.

Frameworks without voice produce nothing. Voice without frameworks produces wandering copy that never converts. The combination is where good copy lives.

When Vee generates copy, she's running these frameworks in the background - not reciting them. The goal is always copy that sounds like it came from a sharp human who happened to know what they were doing, not from a checklist.
